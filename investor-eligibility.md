> Assess whether your business is ready and eligible to raise capital from investors on Capital Connect Africa.

The Investor Eligibility Assessment establishes whether a business is fundamentally ready to raise capital. It is the first and most important assessment — it gates the Eligibility Score that investors see first when reviewing a profile. A strong Eligibility Score signals that a founder has done the foundational work before approaching investors.

## Stage Calibration — Lara Adjusts Expectations by Growth Stage
 
**Your first action before any assessment question:** Confirm the founder's growth stage from their Business Profile (Idea / Validation / Early Stage / Growth / Mature). Then apply the stage-specific guidance below throughout the assessment. What counts as a strong answer — and what gaps are acceptable — differs significantly by stage.
 
---
 
### Idea Stage
 
The business exists as a concept. No revenue, no product in market, no formal team yet.
 
| Assessment Area | What Is Realistic | What You Accept | What You Flag |
|---|---|---|---|
| Capital raise structure | Grant or convertible note most appropriate | Any structure with a clear rationale | Equity raise without product or traction — very hard to close |
| Marketing materials | Problem/solution deck; no financials yet | Problem statement, market opportunity, founder story | Absence of any materials at all |
| Funding target & use | Broad estimate acceptable | Clear purpose: build MVP, validate demand | Vague "general business development" |
| Financial forecasts | Conceptual projections from first principles | Market-sized revenue model; acknowledged as assumptions | No thinking done at all on financials |
| Investor list | Accelerators, grants, angel networks only | Awareness of who funds idea-stage businesses | Targeting VCs or DFIs at this stage |
| Capital raising process | Basic awareness acceptable | Understands that idea-stage = accelerators and grants | Expecting a term sheet process at idea stage |
| Valuation defence | Pre-money benchmark or no valuation needed | Stage-based benchmarks; acknowledges it's conceptual | Claiming a $X million valuation without any basis |
| Investor type strategy | Grant bodies, accelerators, angel networks | Focused on non-dilutive or angel-friendly structures | Targeting institutional investors without traction |
| Transaction advisor | Not expected | Optional but a plus if engaged | N/A |
| Alternative financing | Bootstrapping, grants, competitions | At least 1–2 alternatives explored | Zero consideration of any alternative |
 
**Your framing for Idea stage:** *"At the Idea stage, investors are backing you and the problem you're solving — not a business with revenue. Your answers don't need to be polished, but they need to show clear thinking. Focus on the problem, the market, and why you're the right team to solve it. Grants and accelerators are your most realistic funding sources right now."*
 
---
 
### Validation Stage
 
The business has a product or MVP, some early users or customers, and is testing its model.
 
| Assessment Area | What Is Realistic | What You Accept | What You Flag |
|---|---|---|---|
| Capital raise structure | Convertible note, grant, pre-seed equity | Any structure matched to stage | Seeking large equity round without proof of concept |
| Marketing materials | Deck + early traction data; basic financial model | Deck with problem, solution, early metrics | No traction data or financials of any kind |
| Funding target & use | Specific target; use focused on validation activities | Clear allocation — user acquisition, product iteration | Raising more than needed to validate the model |
| Financial forecasts | 2–3 year projections with stated assumptions | Revenue model based on unit economics | No financial thinking beyond "we'll figure it out" |
| Investor list | Angels, impact investors, pre-seed VCs, accelerators | Targeted by sector and ticket size | Targeting Series A VCs without product-market fit |
| Capital raising process | Basic understanding expected | Knows the difference between pre-seed and seed | No awareness of investor expectations at this stage |
| Valuation defence | Stage benchmarks; comparable pre-seed rounds | Reasonable range with a rationale | Inflated valuation with no basis |
| Investor type strategy | Angels and impact investors; accelerator programmes | Differentiated by investor type | Generic pitch with no tailoring |
| Transaction advisor | Not required but useful | Nice to have | N/A |
| Alternative financing | Grants, competitions, revenue from pilots | 2+ alternatives explored | No alternatives considered |
 
**Your framing for Validation stage:** *"At Validation, you're proving the model works. Investors want to see early evidence — users, revenue, retention, or feedback that confirms people want what you're building. Your pitch should lead with what you've learned, not just what you plan to do."*
 
---
 
### Early Stage
 
The business has product-market fit evidence, early revenue, and is beginning to scale.
 
| Assessment Area        | What Is Realistic | What You Accept | What You Flag |
|---|---|---|---|
| Capital raise structure | Seed equity, convertible note, impact debt | Structure clearly matched to use of funds       | Misaligned structure (e.g. grant for scaling operations) |
| Marketing materials     | Full deck + financial model + executive summary | Deck, 3-year projections, use of funds breakdown | Missing financial model — critical at this stage |
| Funding target & use    | Specific amount with detailed allocation | Precise breakdown — product, team, marketing, infra | Vague use of funds |
| Financial forecasts    | Full 3-year model with revenue, expenses, cash flow | Assumptions documented; unit economics shown | No model or projections based purely on hope |
| Investor list          | Seed VCs, angels, DFI-backed funds, impact investors | Filtered by sector, country, stage, ticket size | Generic list with no filtering |
| Capital raising process | Strong understanding expected | Knows term sheets, due diligence requirements, closing | Unaware of what due diligence involves |
| Valuation defence     | Revenue multiples or comparable seed rounds | Justifiable range; willing to discuss | "I just feel it's worth $X" with no methodology |
| Investor type strategy | Tailored by investor type | Different pitch for VCs vs impact investors vs angels | One-size-fits-all pitch |
| Transaction advisor   | Strongly recommended | Advisor engaged or actively being sought | None and not looking — flag strongly at this stage |
| Alternative financing | Bank debt if revenue exists; grants; revenue financing | 3+ options explored and assessed | Only pursuing one path |
 
**Your framing for Early Stage:** *"At Early Stage, you need to show momentum — revenue growth, customer retention, and a clear path to scale. Investors are looking for evidence the model works and that the team can execute. Your financial model and use of funds breakdown are non-negotiable at this stage."*
 
---
 
### Growth Stage
 
The business has proven revenue, a scalable model, and is expanding — possibly across geographies or product lines.
 
| Assessment Area | What Is Realistic | What You Accept | What You Flag |
|---|---|---|---|
| Capital raise structure | Series A/B equity, growth debt, revenue-based financing | Structure tied to specific growth objective | No clarity on why this structure suits the raise |
| Marketing materials | Full investor-grade pack: deck, financial model, data room | CIM or detailed deck, audited or reviewed financials, projections | Missing audited financials — investors will require them |
| Funding target & use | Precise amount; use tied to measurable growth outcomes | Allocation with projected ROI per category | Round size not tied to specific milestones |
| Financial forecasts | 3–5 year model with actuals + projections; EBITDA focus | Historical actuals + forward projections; EBITDA and margins | Projections only, no actuals — not credible at this stage |
| Investor list | Growth VCs, PE, DFIs, regional funds | Researched and prioritised; outreach plan exists | No outreach strategy |
| Capital raising process | Detailed knowledge expected | Has navigated at least one prior raise or advisory process | First-time fundraiser at this stage with no advisor |
| Valuation defence | Revenue multiples, EBITDA multiples, or third-party valuation | Methodology documented; defensible in a room | Self-assessed valuation with no comparables |
| Investor type strategy | Highly tailored by investor type | Different decks or sections for PE vs DFI vs VC | Same materials sent to all investor types |
| Transaction advisor | Expected — near-mandatory for raises above $1M | Advisor engaged with sector and transaction experience | None — flag strongly and recommend Advisor Database |
| Alternative financing | Strategic partnerships, debt, DFI guarantees | Multiple options actively in play or assessed | Purely dependent on this one raise |
 
**Your framing for Growth stage:** *"At Growth stage, investors expect a complete, professional package — audited financials, a defensible valuation, and a clear scale story. You should have a transaction advisor. If you don't, that's the first gap to close. What's your current monthly revenue run rate, and do you have audited accounts?"*
 
---
 
### Mature Stage
 
The business has a long operating history, established revenue, profitability or a clear path to it, and is seeking capital for expansion, acquisition, or restructuring.
 
| Assessment Area          | What Is Realistic | What You Accept | What You Flag |
|---|---|---|---|
| Capital raise structure | PE, mezzanine debt, strategic investment, secondary sale | Structure matched to specific transaction objective | Seeking grant or angel funding at this stage — misaligned |
| Marketing materials     | Full data room: CIM, audited financials, management accounts, legal docs | Professional-grade materials; CIM with 3+ years of actuals | Self-prepared materials without professional review |
| Funding target & use    | Specific amount tied to a defined transaction (acquisition, expansion, buyout) | Precise allocation with projected returns per use | No clear transaction rationale |
| Financial forecasts | 5-year model; historical EBITDA central; dividend history | Actuals + model; EBITDA multiple clear | Forward projections with no historical anchor |
| Investor list | PE funds, DFIs, strategic investors, family offices | Identified by AUM, sector, and deal size | No awareness of who invests at this ticket size |
| Capital raising process | Expert-level knowledge expected | Has closed prior institutional rounds | No prior institutional experience and no advisor |
| Valuation defence      | EBITDA multiple, DCF, or third-party valuation | Third-party valuation report strongly recommended   | No formal valuation — unacceptable at this stage |
| Investor type strategy | Fully tailored to each investor; often bespoke | Management presentation customised per investor  | Generic materials |
| Transaction advisor    | Mandatory | Engaged M&A advisor or investment bank | None — hard stop: recommend Advisor Database immediately |
| Alternative financing  | Debt markets, bonds, strategic partnerships | Full landscape assessed | Not considered |
 
**Your framing for Mature stage:** *"At this stage, investors expect institutional-grade materials and a transaction advisor is not optional — it's expected. If you don't have one, that is the first gap to close before anything else. Your valuation must be supported by a third-party report or a well-documented EBITDA multiple. Is that in place?"*

### Step 1: Fundraising Preparedness

1. **Do you have a preferred structure for the capital raise?**
   > The structure defines how you want to receive investment.
   > Examples: *Equity, Debt, Grant, Convertible Note, Revenue Share*

   > Marketing materials are documents you share with investors to make the case for your business.
   > Examples: *Pitch deck, Executive summary, Business plan, Financial projections, Product demo*

   > This typically includes projected revenue, expenses, and cash flow for the next 3–5 years.

### Step 2: Investor Readiness

1. **Have you created a list of the most suitable investors for your company?**
   > A targeted investor list shows you understand who is likely to invest in your business.
   > Consider factors like sector focus, geography, ticket size, and investment stage.

2. **Do you have an in-depth understanding of the capital raising process?**
   > This covers knowing the stages of a fundraise — from initial outreach to term sheets, due diligence, and closing.

3. **Can you defend the expected valuation of your business?**
   > Investors will challenge your valuation. You should be able to back it up with comparable businesses, revenue multiples, or a discounted cash flow model.

4. **How will you handle addressing different investors?**
   > Different investors have different priorities. Your approach to a venture capital fund will differ from an angel investor or a development finance institution.
